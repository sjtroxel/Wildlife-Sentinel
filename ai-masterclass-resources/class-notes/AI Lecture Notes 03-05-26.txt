Section 1: The Stakes Are Different (0:10–0:20)

Core message: Using AI as YOUR tool vs. shipping AI to YOUR customers are two completely different games.
The Trust Gap

When AI is your dev tool:

    Wrong answer = annoying, you fix it
    You know when to ignore it

When AI is your customer's experience:

    Wrong answer = support ticket, lost trust, churn
    Customer doesn't know when to ignore it
    Bad AI features are worse than no AI features

The Production Reality Check

    "95% of enterprise AI pilots fail to deliver measurable P&L impact." ² — MIT Media Lab, August 2025

    What this means: MIT surveyed 150 executives, 350 employees, and analyzed 300 real AI deployments. "Measurable P&L impact" means the company could point to actual dollars — revenue gained or costs cut — six months after launch. 95% could not. Only 5% of companies could prove their AI investment worked financially.

    Why it matters: This isn't saying AI doesn't work. It's saying most teams can't prove it worked — because they didn't instrument it. They shipped a feature and hoped. The failure is almost never the model; it's the missing measurement, evaluation, and iteration. As a developer, you're responsible for all of that.

    "42% of companies scrapped most AI initiatives in 2025 — up from 17% in 2024." ³ — S&P Global Market Intelligence, May 2025

    What this means: S&P Global surveyed 1,006 mid-to-senior IT and business decision-makers. In 2024, 17% of companies killed most of their AI projects before reaching production. In 2025, that jumped to 42% — 2.5x more companies pulling the plug, year over year. On average, each company scrapped 46% of its AI proof-of-concepts before they reached users.

    Why it matters: More AI investment is producing more failures, not fewer. The industry hasn't figured this out yet. Also a practical note: if you ever need to pitch an AI feature internally, you're walking into a room where nearly half of similar initiatives just got cancelled. How you frame the business case — and the instrumentation behind it — matters.

The gap between a working demo and a reliable product feature is enormous. The root cause is almost never the model — it's wrong pattern selection, skipped evaluation infrastructure, and zero failure planning.
What This Week Is About

Not "how do you add a chatbot" — anyone can do that in 30 minutes.

It's: how do you add an AI feature that your customers will actually trust?
Section 2: AI Feature Pattern Survey (0:20–0:35)

Core message: "Add a chatbot" is the only AI feature most devs think of. There are seven patterns. Know them so you can match the right one to the right problem.
The Seven Patterns

1. Chatbot / Conversational Interface (12-Factor #1: Natural Language to Tool Calls)

    Natural language → structured action or answer

    Best for: customer support, onboarding, FAQs

    Pitfall: open-ended questions → hallucination risk

    Example: Intercom Fin — 66% average resolution rate across 6,000+ customers; 20%+ of customers exceed 80% ¹

        What this means: "Resolution rate" means the percentage of customer conversations that start AND end with the AI — no human agent involved. 66% means roughly 2 out of 3 customer conversations are handled entirely by the AI. The 6,000+ customer sample means this is a real-world average, not a cherry-picked case study.

        Why it matters: Two things. First, this is the benchmark for what "working" looks like — not "does it respond" but "does it actually resolve." Second, the flip side: the best AI support tool in production still fails to resolve 1 in 3 conversations. Build for that 34% from day one.

2. Intelligent Search

    Semantic understanding of user intent vs. keyword matching
    Best for: knowledge bases, documentation, product catalogs
    Pitfall: relevance is hard to measure, feels magical until it doesn't
    Example: Notion AI search, GitHub code search

3. Content Generation / Drafting

    AI writes a first draft, human reviews
    Best for: emails, reports, summaries, code suggestions
    Pitfall: users accept bad output without reading → trust erodes when it ships
    Example: GitHub Copilot, Cursor, email assistants

4. Recommendations

    Pattern recognition over user behavior or content
    Best for: "you might also like," surfacing buried features, next-best-action
    Pitfall: requires data; cold start problem
    Example: Netflix, Spotify Discover Weekly

5. Intelligent Routing

    AI decides where to send a request — to a human, a department, a tool
    Best for: support ticket triage, workflow automation, multi-agent orchestration
    Pitfall: miscategorization is invisible until it causes a real problem

6. Human Handoff

    AI handles until it can't, then passes to a human WITH context
    Best for: any high-stakes workflow (sales, healthcare, finance)
    This is a pattern, not a fallback — design for it from day one
    Example: bank software workflow — AI books meeting, generates summary for human rep

7. Data Enrichment

    AI automatically adds context, metadata, or intelligence to existing records — silently, in the background, at the data layer

    Best for: CRM records (job title, company size, tech stack), support tickets (auto-tagging intent/sentiment/urgency), digital assets (keywords, summaries), product catalogs (descriptions, categories)

    Key differentiator from the other six: users usually never see it directly — the enriched data just makes search, routing, and recommendations work better downstream

    Pitfall: silent failure — a wrong chatbot answer is caught immediately; a wrong enriched field lives quietly in your database corrupting decisions for months ⁴

    Example: Zendesk Intelligent Triage auto-enriches every ticket with intent, sentiment, and language on arrival — saves ~45 seconds per ticket; ~120 hours/month for an enterprise retailer ⁵. HubSpot Breeze Intelligence auto-fills contact and company records with firmographics, technographics, and buyer intent signals ⁶

        What this means: Before AI triage, when a support ticket arrives, a human agent reads it, determines what it's about, how upset the customer is, and manually tags it before routing — 30–60 seconds of cognitive overhead per ticket. Zendesk's enrichment does all of that automatically at arrival. The ~120 hours/month figure assumes high ticket volume typical of an enterprise retailer.

        Why it matters: 120 hours/month is roughly one full-time employee's time — and this is a feature the customer never sees. They submit their ticket, it gets routed faster and more accurately, and they never know an AI touched it before a human did. Invisible to users, concrete business value, measurable from day one. That's the enrichment pattern's signature.

The Co-Pilot Principle

The AI features that ship successfully share one trait: they embed into existing workflows instead of forcing users into new paradigms.

Ask yourself: "Does this feature replace something the user already does, or does it add friction?"
Section 3: RAG Fundamentals + Chunking (0:35–0:55)

Core message: RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) is how you give AI access to YOUR product's knowledge. It's not complicated conceptually. But chunking strategy makes or breaks the whole system — and most teams get it wrong.

    12-Factor #3: Own Your Context Window — RAG is fundamentally about deciding what information earns a spot in the LLM's context. Every chunking and retrieval decision is a context window decision.

Why RAG Exists

LLMs are trained on public data up to a cutoff. Your product has:

    Your docs, knowledge base, pricing, policies
    Customer history
    Internal domain knowledge

You can't fine-tune a model every time something changes. RAG solves this by retrieving relevant context at query time.
The RAG Stack (Explain Each Step)

User Query
    ↓
Embedding Model (converts query to vector)
    ↓
Vector Database (finds similar chunks)
    ↓
Retrieved Chunks (passed into LLM context)
    ↓
LLM generates answer grounded in YOUR content

Key terms to define:

    Embedding: converting text to a numerical representation that captures meaning
    Vector: just an array of numbers — you've already used one. [255, 128, 0] is a vector that represents orange. Those three numbers are coordinates in color space. Colors that look similar have coordinates that are close together. An embedding works the same way, except instead of 3 dimensions capturing color properties, you have ~1,500 dimensions capturing meaning properties — topic, tone, formality, domain, sentiment. The dimensions aren't human-interpretable (you can't look at dimension 742 and call it "the formality axis" — the model learned them). But the geometry is real: similar meaning → similar coordinates → close together in vector space.
    Embedding models and dimensions: different models use different numbers of dimensions — that's a design choice, not a flaw. RGB uses 3 dimensions; CMYK uses 4. Same colors, totally different coordinate systems — and you can't mix them. Embedding models are the same: qwen3-embedding (Ollama) uses 2,048 dimensions; nomic-embed-text uses 768. More dimensions = more expressive but more storage and slower search. The critical rule: you must use the same model for indexing and for querying. If you embed your docs with model A and your queries with model B, you're comparing RGB coordinates to CMYK coordinates — the math is meaningless. Switching models means re-embedding your entire corpus. The gains from higher dimensions are also not linear — think audio quality: too few dimensions and the model can't distinguish subtle meaning differences ("cancel my subscription" and "cancel before billing" land in nearly the same spot). The sweet spot for most use cases is 384–1,024 dimensions. Higher-dim models (2,048+) help most with edge cases: ambiguous queries, very short queries, or queries that straddle two topics. For clear, specific queries ("what's your return policy?"), a 384-dim model does just as well.
    Vector database: a database optimized for similarity search on embeddings — "find the arrays that are closest to this array"
    Retrieval: finding the chunks most similar to the user's query
    Grounding: forcing the LLM to base its answer on retrieved content, not training data

Chunking Strategies (The Part Teams Get Wrong)

This is where most RAG implementations fail or succeed.

Fixed-Size Chunking

    Split every N tokens (e.g., 512 tokens), with overlap (e.g., 50 tokens)
    Simple, fast, easy to implement
    Problem: cuts mid-sentence, mid-paragraph — loses coherence
    Good for: quick prototypes, evenly structured content

Semantic Chunking

    Split at natural breakpoints: sentences, paragraphs, sections
    Preserves coherent units of meaning
    Problem: chunks vary in size; can be expensive to compute
    Good for: prose-heavy docs, knowledge bases

Document-Structure Chunking

    Respect document hierarchy: H1 > H2 > H3 > paragraphs
    Keep headings attached to content
    Problem: requires knowing your document structure
    Good for: structured documentation, policies, product specs

The Core Tradeoff:
Chunk Size 	Retrieval Precision 	Context Quality
Small 	High (specific) 	Low (loses context)
Large 	Low (noisy) 	High (keeps context)

There's no perfect chunk size — you tune it for your content type.
Evaluating Retrieval Quality

Don't just evaluate the final answer. Evaluate the retrieval separately:

    Are the right chunks being retrieved?
    Is the answer actually grounded in the retrieved chunks?
    What happens when the answer isn't in the docs? (It should say "I don't know")

Quick eval test: Take 20 real questions your users ask. Run them through RAG. Read the retrieved chunks. Do they contain the answer?
Vector DB Options (Quick Survey)

    Pinecone: managed, easy to start, scales well — production default for many teams
    pgvector: if you're already on Postgres, add vector search without new infra
    Chroma: local, open source, good for development
    Weaviate, Qdrant: open source options with good self-hosting

    Don't overthink the vector DB. Start with what integrates into your existing stack.

Section 4: Human Handoff Architecture (1:05–1:15)

Core message: Most AI features are designed as all-or-nothing. That's wrong. Human handoff isn't a fallback — it's a first-class feature you design for from day one.
The Three-Zone Model

High Confidence → AI handles fully
Low Confidence → AI + human review
Out of Scope → Route to human with AI-generated summary

Live Case Study: Bank Software (Real Architecture)

Here's what the actual hard decisions look like.

The agent topology: (12-Factor #10: Small, Focused Agents)

Customer message
    ↓
Triage Agent (intent classification + complexity scoring)
    ↓              ↓                    ↓
Domain Agent   Booking Agent      Escalation Path
(answers)      (MCP actions)      (handoff packet → human rep)

Routing decision: LLM-based vs. code-based (12-Factor #8: Own Your Control Flow)
The triage agent can route using LLM reasoning (flexible, but agents can hallucinate next actions or create circular dependencies) or using a deterministic state machine where the LLM only makes bounded decisions at explicit transition points (e.g., output "route_to_calendar_agent" — nothing else). Production reality: use code-based routing. Treat agent outputs as control instructions, not suggestions. Keep orchestration logic outside the model where it's verifiable and testable.

Confidence thresholds — don't do this by feel:
Self-reported LLM confidence ({"confidence": 0.85, "can_handle": true}) is a starting point, not a solution. LLMs are systematically overconfident due to RLHF — reward models during training bias toward high-confidence outputs regardless of correctness. Your 0.85 is not calibrated to reality. The fix: treat self-reported scores as qualitative signals, then empirically calibrate against real outcomes. Run the system, track escalation decisions vs. actual resolution success, adjust thresholds from data. A threshold of 0.70 means nothing until you've seen what 0.70 actually looks like in your domain. For static bounded tasks, temperature scaling post-hoc is a practical technique for restoring calibration (can achieve near-zero calibration error on classification tasks).

    What "RLHF overconfidence" means: RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) is how models are fine-tuned after initial training. Human raters score responses, and the model learns what gets rated highly. Problem: raters tend to prefer confident-sounding answers. So the model learns that sounding confident gets rewarded — regardless of whether it actually knows the answer. The confidence number it reports reflects learned signaling behavior, not a calibrated probability.

    Why it matters: A model saying "confidence: 0.85" might mean it's right 60% of the time in your domain, or 95%. You don't know until you measure it. Designing handoff thresholds by feel — "0.7 seems right" — is how you end up with an AI that confidently handles things it gets wrong, and escalates things it would have resolved just fine.

    What "temperature scaling" means: A post-processing technique that fits a single adjustment parameter to a small calibration dataset. You're not retraining the model — just rescaling its confidence outputs so they match empirical accuracy. "Near-zero calibration error" means if the model says 80% confident, it's actually right ~80% of the time.

    Why it matters: Low effort, high payoff for any classification task (intent detection, routing, triage). You can take an overconfident model and make it reliably calibrated with a small calibration set and a one-line post-processing step.

    Live example — the nuclear option: If you can't trust self-reported confidence, stop asking for it entirely. The vibeathon judging pipeline running in this class takes this approach: the LLM never produces a score. It only collects discrete evidence items (+3, -2, etc.) with file-level references. The final score is computed by a formula — net_impact / sqrt(total_items) — that the LLM has no input on. Confidence is derived from how much evidence was found, not from what the model says about itself. Sparse evidence → score gets pulled toward 50 (empirical Bayes shrinkage). This is a valid architectural pattern for any high-stakes LLM scoring decision: separate evidence collection from score computation, and derive confidence from evidence density rather than self-report.

    What this means: The formula net_impact / sqrt(total_items) normalizes a score by how much evidence supported it. If the LLM found 4 evidence items adding up to +6, you get 6/√4 = 3. If it only found 1 item adding up to +6, you get 6/√1 = 6 — but that single item gets less weight elsewhere in the system because sparse evidence = lower confidence. "Bayesian shrinkage toward 50" means: when evidence is thin, the score gets pulled toward the midpoint (uncertain), rather than swinging to an extreme based on one data point.

    Why it matters: The core architectural insight generalizes beyond judging: don't ask the LLM for one big holistic score — break the judgment into smaller, bounded decisions it can make more accurately, then aggregate mathematically. Asking a model to rate something 1–100 is a hard, unconstrained problem. Asking it "is this a +1, +2, or +3 impact item?" is a much smaller, more reliable judgment. The LLM is still scoring — just in increments where it's less likely to go wrong. The final number emerges from math, not from the model guessing at a total. This applies anywhere LLM scoring matters: content moderation severity, risk triage, diagnostic support, code review impact.

MCP tool design — atomic, verifiable, idempotent: (12-Factor #4: Tools Are Structured Outputs)
Three non-negotiables:

    Atomic — one tool does one thing. BookMeeting(params) is a tool. HandleCustomerInquiry(params) is not.
    Structured error returns (12-Factor #9: Compact Errors into Context) — errors go in the result object with isError: true and a human-readable message, not as protocol-level exceptions. The agent needs to see the failure to handle it. A bad error message (KeyError: 'id') makes recovery impossible. A good one ("No available slots found for requested date") gives the agent something to work with.
    Idempotent — agents retry on timeout. If BookMeeting gets called twice because the first call timed out, it must handle the duplicate gracefully. Use idempotency keys. For anything with side effects (charges, calendar writes, CRM updates), use a transactional outbox pattern — write to an outbox table in the same transaction as the business logic; dispatch separately.

The handoff packet is a product decision, not a technical one: (12-Factor #7: Contact Humans with Tool Calls — the escalation is just another tool call; the packet is its structured output. 12-Factor #6: Launch/Pause/Resume — the workflow pauses here and resumes when the human acts.)
The two classic failure modes are "amnesia" (agent escalates, human rep has zero context, customer repeats everything) and "cold transfer" (rep gets a raw chat log, spends 3–5 minutes just orienting). The fix is a structured packet the rep can act on in 30 seconds:

    Intent — what did the customer actually want?
    Key entities — account number, issue type, dates mentioned
    Attempted solutions + results — what was tried, what worked, what didn't
    Escalation reason — why was this handed off? (confidence breach, explicit request, detected complexity)
    Recommended next action — AI's suggested path forward

Don't send the raw conversation log. Don't free-form it. The format is a conversation with your reps: "What's the minimum you need to walk in already briefed?" Klarna learned this the hard way — their initial AI-first approach had reps swimming in unstructured context before they moved to structured packets with real-time CRM data pulled at escalation time.

    What the Klarna example means: Klarna (the buy-now-pay-later company) deployed an aggressive AI-first support strategy in early 2024. When escalation was needed, reps received the full, unstructured conversation log. They had to read through the whole thing to figure out what the customer wanted, what had been tried, and where things stood — 3–5 minutes of orienting before they could even start helping. They later rebuilt the handoff to deliver a structured summary with CRM context pulled at escalation time.

    Why it matters: A raw conversation log is not a handoff packet. The packet format should answer one question: "What's the minimum a rep needs to walk in already briefed?" If they have to read more than 30 seconds to orient, the design has failed — and over time, they'll stop reading it at all, defeating the entire point of the handoff architecture.

Where it actually breaks in production:

    Triage agent mis-classifies edge cases → domain agent gets in over its head → customer gets a confident wrong answer
    MCP tool fails without a structured error → agent assumes success → hallucinated confirmation sent to customer
    Two agents write to shared state concurrently → race condition → final state depends on write timing, not logic (fix: single-writer patterns or distributed locks) (12-Factor #5: Unify Execution State and Business State)
    Confidence threshold calibrated once at launch and never revisited → drifts as usage patterns change → silent degradation
    Handoff packet too verbose → reps stop reading it → defeats the whole purpose

The state machine test: (12-Factor #12: Make Your Agent a Stateless Reducer)
If you can't draw the workflow as a state machine with explicit transitions and failure paths at every node, the design isn't done. Every state needs: what triggers it, what can go wrong, and where it goes when it fails. Partial success (steps 1–3 succeeded, step 4 failed) is a real state that needs an explicit recovery path.
Section 5: Ship It Right — Flags, Gateways, and Monitoring (1:15–1:35)

Core message: The difference between teams that ship AI features successfully and those that don't is eval infrastructure, feature flags, and monitoring — not the model.
Feature Flags Are Non-Negotiable

Every AI feature should be behind a feature flag.

Why: AI features fail in unexpected ways in production. When something goes wrong, you need a kill switch that takes 30 seconds, not a deployment.

Pattern: release to 5% → monitor → 25% → monitor → 100%
The AI Gateway Layer

An AI gateway sits between your application and the LLM provider. It handles:

    Cost tracking — know what you're spending per feature
    Rate limiting — protect against runaway costs
    Model routing — fallback if your primary model is down
    Guardrails — catch bad outputs before they reach users
    Observability — log every request/response for debugging

Options: LiteLLM (open source, easiest entry), Portkey (managed, 200+ LLMs), Cloudflare AI Gateway (free tier, Cloudflare ecosystem)

    You don't need a gateway on day one for a side project. You do need one for anything customer-facing at scale.

Eval-Driven Development

The teams that ship reliable AI features do evals BEFORE launch and CONTINUOUSLY in production.

Offline evals (before launch):

    Build a test set of questions/expected answers from your domain
    Run your RAG pipeline against it
    Score: did it retrieve the right chunks? Did it answer correctly?

Online evals (after launch):

    Track thumbs up/down (underrated — users will use it)
    Track fallback rate (how often does it escalate to human?)
    Track latency (AI features often feel slow — acceptable threshold depends on context)
    Track cost per query

The Metrics That Matter
Metric 	What It Tells You
User satisfaction (thumbs) 	Is it actually helpful?
Fallback rate 	Is confidence calibrated correctly?
Retrieval precision 	Are the right chunks being surfaced?
Latency 	Is it fast enough to feel useful?
Cost per query 	Is it economically viable?
Prompt Management (12-Factor #2: Own Your Prompts)

Prompts are production code. Treat them that way.

    Version control your prompts
    Test prompt changes like you test code changes
    Never push prompt changes to 100% without eval

    A/B testing prompts is one of the highest-leverage things you can do once you're in production.

12-Factor Agents Reference

The 12-Factor Agents framework (humanlayer.dev / github.com/humanlayer/12-factor-agents) is analogous to the original 12-Factor App for web services — a set of principles for building reliable, production-grade LLM applications. 11 of the 12 factors are referenced throughout this outline. Worth assigning as optional reading.
