IPBES GLOBAL ASSESSMENT REPORT ON BIODIVERSITY AND ECOSYSTEM SERVICES
Summary for Policymakers — 2019
Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services

OVERVIEW

The Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES) released its first Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services in May 2019. Representatives from 132 IPBES member nations adopted the Summary for Policymakers in Paris between April 29 and May 4, 2019. The 40-page summary represented the most comprehensive planetary assessment of biodiversity and ecosystem services ever conducted, drawing on approximately 15,000 scientific and government sources.

The central headline finding: approximately one million animal and plant species are currently threatened with extinction — more than at any other point in human history.

KEY FINDINGS

Nature's Unprecedented Decline

The natural world is deteriorating globally at rates unprecedented in human history. This decline accelerates and is directly linked to human activities. The rate of global change in nature during the past 50 years is unprecedented in human history. The threat to species is accelerating.

Around one million species already face extinction, many within decades, unless action is taken to reduce the intensity of drivers of biodiversity loss.

The direct drivers of change in nature, ranked in descending order of impact globally over the last five decades, are:
1. Changes in land and sea use (primary driver)
2. Direct exploitation of organisms (hunting, fishing, logging, harvesting)
3. Climate change
4. Pollution
5. Invasive alien species

These five drivers are interacting and often mutually reinforcing, and are themselves driven by fundamental indirect societal factors including production and consumption patterns, human population dynamics and trends, trade, technological innovation, and local through global governance.

Land and Ecosystem Alteration

More than 75 percent of Earth's land surface has been significantly altered by human actions, and more than 85 percent of wetland area has been lost. This alteration is primary cause of land-based biodiversity loss globally. Since 1980, greenhouse gas emissions have doubled, raising average global temperatures by at least 0.7 degrees Celsius — with climate change already impacting nature from the genes to biomes level.

More than a third of the world's land surface and nearly 75 percent of freshwater resources are devoted to crop or livestock production. Urban areas have more than doubled since 1992. Plastic pollution has increased tenfold since 1980.

Over 33 percent of marine fish stocks were being harvested at unsustainable levels in 2015; 60 percent were harvested at the maximum sustainable level, leaving just 7 percent harvested at less than their maximum sustainable level.

Land degradation has reduced the productivity of 23 percent of global land surface. Up to USD 577 billion in annual global crop output is at risk from pollinator loss.

Ecosystem Status

Native species in terrestrial areas have fallen by at least 20 percent on average since 1900. More than 40 percent of amphibian species, almost 33 percent of reef-forming corals and more than a third of all marine mammals are threatened. The picture is less clear for insect species, but available evidence supports a tentative estimate of 10 percent being threatened.

At least 680 vertebrate species had been driven to extinction since the 16th century, and more than 9 percent of all domesticated breeds of mammals used for food and agriculture had become extinct by 2016, with at least 1,000 more breeds still threatened.

Global biomass of wild mammals has fallen by 82 percent. Natural ecosystem extent and condition have declined by 47 percent, on average, relative to their estimated natural baselines. Three-quarters of the land-based environment and about 66 percent of the marine environment have been significantly altered by human actions.

DIRECT DRIVERS OF BIODIVERSITY LOSS IN DETAIL

Land Use Change

Changes in land use — conversion of habitats for agriculture, forestry, and development — constitute the single greatest direct driver of nature loss in terrestrial and freshwater ecosystems. Agriculture has expanded at the expense of natural ecosystems such as tropical forests, savannas, wetlands, and grasslands. The 2019 Global Assessment identified industrial agriculture as the primary driver of biodiversity collapse globally.

The conversion and degradation of forest, wetland, and grassland habitats continues at an alarming pace. Tropical deforestation is particularly damaging because tropical forests contain more than half of all terrestrial species, many of which are endemic to small geographic areas. Deforestation drives immediate habitat loss and fragmentation, which isolates populations, reduces genetic diversity, and eventually leads to local extinctions.

Overexploitation

The direct exploitation of organisms through hunting, fishing, logging, and collecting for trade represents the second greatest driver of biodiversity loss globally. Commercial fishing has reduced the biomass of fish and marine mammals by approximately 60 percent since the 1800s. Overfishing is identified as the main driver of mass species extinction in marine ecosystems, currently pushing over one-third of sharks and rays toward extinction.

Illegal wildlife trade — including poaching, bushmeat hunting, and collection for the exotic pet trade — pushes species toward extinction. Nearly 30 percent of bird extinctions result from hunting and trapping. African elephant populations have fallen from 1.7 million in 1979 to under 400,000, driven by ivory poaching alongside habitat loss.

Climate Change

Climate change is the third-ranked driver of biodiversity loss globally, but its relative impact is growing and will likely overtake land use change this century. The IPBES assessment found climate change already contributes to ecosystem change at all scales.

Warm-water coral reefs are extremely sensitive to warming ocean temperatures. Even limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius will eliminate 70–90 percent of today's warm-water coral reefs. Caribbean coral reefs are projected to be lost within the century at current warming trajectories. Coral reefs host thousands of species — fish, invertebrates, and algae — and provide coastal protection and food security services for hundreds of millions of people.

Rising sea levels threaten coastal wetland habitats. Melting sea ice destroys polar habitat. Climate change alters seasonal timing (phenology), disrupting synchronized interactions between species such as pollinator-plant relationships. Every 1 degree Celsius of warming contracts suitable habitat ranges for cold-adapted and specialist species.

Pollution

Pollution — including nutrient runoff, pesticides, heavy metals, plastics, light, and noise — significantly degrades ecosystems and harms species. Nutrient pollution creates oxygen-depleted dead zones in coastal waters. Pesticide use reduces insect abundance, affecting pollinators and insectivorous birds throughout food webs. Plastic pollution has increased tenfold since 1980 and now affects at least 267 species including marine turtles, seabirds, and marine mammals.

Air pollution from industrial and agricultural activities releases sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides that create acid rain, damaging freshwater and forest ecosystems. Noise pollution affects at least 55 marine species, with seismic exploration and naval sonar causing documented cetacean mortality and behavioral disruption.

Invasive Alien Species

Invasive alien species represent one of the top five direct drivers of biodiversity loss globally, with impacts particularly severe on islands and in freshwater systems. Invasive species drive local and global extinction through competitive exclusion, predation, disease transmission, and hybridization. The chytrid fungus Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis — spread by the global amphibian trade — has devastated over 500 amphibian species and caused approximately 90 extinctions, representing the greatest disease-driven loss of biodiversity in recorded history.

Invasive species are increasing in prevalence as global trade, tourism, and transport accelerate their spread. Once established, invasive species are rarely eradicated and their impacts are largely irreversible.

ECOSYSTEM SERVICES AND HUMAN WELL-BEING

Nature provides many crucial contributions to people — food, clean water, air purification, disease regulation, climate regulation, cultural and spiritual value. The degradation of natural systems undermines these contributions in ways that disproportionately harm vulnerable populations.

Global food security is increasingly at risk. Up to USD 577 billion in annual global crop output depends on animal pollination. Freshwater availability is declining in many regions as watersheds are degraded. Coastal protection provided by mangroves, salt marshes, and coral reefs is being lost as these habitats are degraded. Climate regulation services provided by intact forests are being eroded by deforestation.

The assessment emphasizes that the health, livelihoods, food security, and quality of life of people worldwide are affected by current and expected future declines in nature and its contributions to people.

INDIRECT DRIVERS

The five direct drivers of nature loss are themselves driven by societal factors. The IPBES assessment identified key indirect drivers:

Production and consumption patterns: Demand for food, materials, and energy shapes what ecosystems are converted and exploited. Global material consumption of biomass, fossil fuels, metals and minerals roughly tripled since 1970. Per-capita consumption in high-income nations far exceeds planetary boundaries.

Trade: International trade externalize environmental costs. Biodiversity loss in supplier nations is driven by demand from consumer nations. Agricultural expansion in tropical biodiversity hotspots is often driven by export commodity production.

Governance and institutional failures: Lack of coordination across policy sectors (agriculture, fisheries, trade, development) results in policies that inadvertently subsidize nature loss. Perverse subsidies to harmful industries total trillions of dollars annually.

CONCLUSIONS AND REQUIRED TRANSFORMATIVE CHANGE

The assessment concludes that goals for conserving and sustainably using nature and achieving sustainability cannot be met by current trajectories. The report notes that business-as-usual approaches, even with modest improvements, are insufficient to halt biodiversity loss.

Meeting global goals requires transformative change — fundamental, system-wide reorganization across technological, economic, and social factors, including paradigms, goals, and values. Transformative change is not incremental improvement but structural reform of the drivers of biodiversity loss.

The window for action is urgent. The 2050 Global Biodiversity Framework vision of living in harmony with nature requires immediate, ambitious action across all sectors of society and economy. Biodiversity loss is not only an environmental problem — it is a development, economic, security, social, and moral issue.
