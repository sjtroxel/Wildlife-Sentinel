WWF LIVING PLANET REPORT 2024
World Wide Fund for Nature
Biennial Assessment of Global Wildlife Populations and Ecosystem Health

OVERVIEW

The Living Planet Report is published biennially by the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) beginning in 1998. It serves as the world's leading science-based analysis of the health of the planet and the impact of human activity on wildlife and ecosystems. The report relies on the Living Planet Index (LPI) — a measure of the state of global biological diversity based on population trends of vertebrate species — as its primary metric.

The 2024 Living Planet Report presents the most alarming findings in the report's history. The global Living Planet Index shows a 73 percent average decline in monitored wildlife populations between 1970 and 2020. This analysis is based on approximately 35,000 populations representing 5,495 species of mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, and fish worldwide.

KEY FINDINGS — 2024 REPORT

The 73 percent average decline in the LPI as of 2020 surpasses the 67 percent decline predicted in the 2016 report. The pace of population decline is not slowing; in many regions and taxonomic groups it is accelerating.

The most severe declines are observed in freshwater species (83 percent average decline since 1970), followed by terrestrial species (69 percent) and marine species (56 percent). Freshwater ecosystems occupy only 1 percent of Earth's surface but support approximately one-third of all vertebrate species, making their accelerating decline particularly alarming for biodiversity.

Latin America and the Caribbean show the steepest regional declines — populations of monitored vertebrates in tropical subregions of South and Central America have fallen by approximately 89 percent since 1970. Africa shows declines of approximately 66 percent, Asia-Pacific approximately 60 percent, and North America and Europe show more modest declines, partly reflecting earlier baseline biodiversity loss in those regions.

PRIMARY THREATS TO WILDLIFE POPULATIONS

Habitat Loss and Degradation

Habitat loss and degradation — driven primarily by agriculture and land conversion — remain the dominant threats to wildlife globally. Industrial agriculture, including crop production and livestock grazing, now occupies approximately 38 percent of Earth's terrestrial surface. The conversion of tropical forests, savannas, wetlands, and grasslands for agriculture and infrastructure eliminates habitat for thousands of species simultaneously.

Over 17,000 species face habitat loss by 2050 as agriculture continues expanding. A 2023 Science Advances analysis determined that at least 30 percent of land must be actively protected and ecologically restored by 2030 to meet biodiversity goals under the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework.

Deforestation is a particularly acute driver of biodiversity loss in tropical regions. Rainforest conversion for oil palm plantations in Southeast Asia threatens orangutans, tigers, pygmy elephants, and thousands of other species. Amazon deforestation for soy cultivation and cattle ranching threatens jaguars, tapirs, giant anteaters, and hundreds of endemic bird and reptile species. Since 1970, primary tropical forest loss has tracked closely with vertebrate population declines in those regions.

Overexploitation

Direct exploitation — hunting, fishing, poaching, and wildlife trade — represents the second major threat driver measured by the Living Planet Index. A 2017 World Wildlife Fund study attributed 60 percent of biodiversity loss to feed crop cultivation for farm animals. The 2019 IPBES assessment found overfishing the main driver of marine species mass extinction, with global fish populations declining 38 percent since 1970.

Trophy hunting of large mammals, poaching for ivory, bushmeat hunting, and collection for the exotic pet trade continue pushing vulnerable species toward extinction. Nearly 30 percent of bird extinctions result from hunting and trapping. Iconic megafauna populations have plummeted: African elephant populations have fallen from 1.7 million in 1979 to under 400,000; lion populations fell from approximately 450,000 to 25,000; cheetah populations declined from 45,000 to approximately 12,000; tigers from 50,000 to approximately 3,000 wild individuals.

Climate Change

Climate change is an increasingly important driver of wildlife population declines and is expected to become the dominant threat this century. Every degree of warming contracts suitable habitat ranges for heat-sensitive species, disrupts seasonal timing, and intensifies extreme weather events that devastate local populations.

Coral reefs, which support approximately 25 percent of all marine species despite covering less than 1 percent of the ocean floor, are highly sensitive to temperature anomalies. Mass coral bleaching events — which were once rare — now occur regularly on the Great Barrier Reef, Caribbean reefs, and Indo-Pacific reefs. Even limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius is projected to eliminate 70–90 percent of remaining coral reefs globally.

Polar and mountain species face the most direct habitat loss from warming temperatures. Arctic sea ice has declined more than 50 percent since first satellite records, threatening polar bears, ringed seals, walruses, and the entire ice-dependent food web. Mountain glaciers retreat, drying rivers and streams that support endemic freshwater species.

Pollution

Pollution — including agricultural runoff, pesticides, plastics, industrial chemicals, light, and noise — significantly depresses wildlife population numbers. Pesticide contamination has been implicated in insect biomass declines of 75 percent in German nature reserves over 25 years and is a leading driver of global pollinator loss.

Plastic pollution affects at least 267 marine species including sea turtles, seabirds, and marine mammals through ingestion, entanglement, and toxic chemical exposure. Nutrient runoff from agriculture creates coastal dead zones, eliminating fish and invertebrate populations in affected areas.

FRESHWATER ECOSYSTEM CRISIS

The Living Planet Report highlights freshwater biodiversity as the most severely affected domain. Since 1970, freshwater vertebrate populations have declined by 83 percent on average — more than twice the rate of terrestrial or marine species declines.

Freshwater species face compounding pressures: water extraction for agriculture and urban use reduces river and lake volumes; damming disrupts migratory fish routes and alters sediment and nutrient flow; agricultural runoff creates toxic or eutrophic conditions; wetland drainage eliminates critical habitat.

Migratory freshwater fish populations have declined 76 percent since 1970. Megafish — freshwater fish exceeding 30 kg — have declined 94 percent globally, with 16 species declared extinct in 2020. Freshwater turtles, river dolphins, and amphibians show similarly alarming trajectories.

Wetland loss is particularly acute. More than 85 percent of global wetland area has been lost since 1700, and losses continue at a rate 3 times faster than forests. Wetlands provide critical services: water filtration, flood regulation, carbon sequestration, and habitat for millions of species.

MARINE ECOSYSTEM STATUS

The world's oceans have lost approximately 40–50 percent of seagrass meadow coverage and 50 percent of coral reef coverage in the past century. Commercial fishing has reduced biomass of fish and marine mammals by approximately 60 percent since the 1800s.

A 2021 Nature study found 71 percent of oceanic shark and ray populations destroyed by overfishing from 1970–2018, with 24 of 31 studied species threatened with extinction. Coral reef sharks and rays face particularly severe decline — nearly two-thirds face extinction risk.

Marine mammals show dramatic declines from historical baselines. Wild marine mammal biomass has fallen approximately 80 percent from pre-industrial levels. While some species have partially recovered following hunting bans (blue whales, humpback whales), others remain critically depleted (North Atlantic right whale: approximately 340 individuals remain).

BIODIVERSITY HOTSPOTS UNDER THREAT

Tropical forests contain more than half of all terrestrial species, making them the most species-rich ecosystems on Earth. The Amazon, Congo Basin, and Southeast Asian rainforests are all experiencing ongoing deforestation and degradation. Species endemic to these regions have nowhere else to go when their habitat is destroyed.

Coral reef systems — including the Great Barrier Reef, Caribbean reefs, Coral Triangle, and Indian Ocean reefs — face simultaneous threats from warming, acidification, pollution, and destructive fishing. Species dependent on coral reefs include parrotfish, sea turtles, manta rays, sharks, and thousands of invertebrates.

Freshwater systems in large river basins support extraordinary biodiversity: the Amazon, Mekong, Congo, Ganges-Brahmaputra, and other major rivers host endemic fish, river dolphins, giant otters, and hundreds of reptile and amphibian species. Damming, water extraction, and pollution threaten these systems globally.

LIVING PLANET INDEX HISTORICAL TRAJECTORY

The Living Planet Index provides a longitudinal view of average vertebrate population change since 1970:
- 1998 report (first LPR): approximately 30 percent decline since 1970
- 2014 report: 52 percent average decline since 1970
- 2016 report: 58 percent decline since 1970
- 2018 report: 60 percent decline since 1970
- 2020 report: 68 percent decline since 1970
- 2022 report: 69 percent decline since 1970 (32,000 populations, 5,230 species)
- 2024 report: 73 percent decline since 1970 (35,000 populations, 5,495 species)

The consistent direction and accelerating magnitude of this trend represents unambiguous evidence of global biodiversity crisis.

CONNECTIONS BETWEEN WILDLIFE AND DISASTERS

Natural disasters — including wildfires, tropical storms, floods, droughts, and coral bleaching — interact with chronic biodiversity loss threats in complex ways.

Wildfires increasingly threaten wildlife directly through mortality and habitat destruction, and indirectly by fragmenting remaining habitat and stressing already-depleted populations. The 2019–2020 Australian bushfire season killed or displaced an estimated 3 billion animals and accelerated several species toward extinction status.

Tropical storms cause immediate wildlife mortality and habitat destruction, and their increasing intensity under climate change threatens coastal and island species with limited range. Storm surge destroys nesting habitat for sea turtles, shorebirds, and mangrove-dependent species. Freshwater flooding can displace and kill terrestrial animals, and saline intrusion from storm surge can devastate freshwater ecosystems.

Droughts directly reduce prey availability, concentrate predators and prey around limited water sources, and stress populations already reduced by other threats. Prolonged drought can cause local extinction of small populations by eliminating the minimum viable habitat area and water resources needed for survival.

Coral bleaching events — driven by anomalously warm sea surface temperatures — are among the most directly observable impacts of climate change on biodiversity. When bleaching is severe and prolonged, it causes mass coral mortality, eliminating the structural habitat that supports hundreds of associated species.

CONCLUSIONS AND CALL TO ACTION

The 2024 Living Planet Report concludes that the global biodiversity crisis demands urgent, systemic change in how humanity relates to the natural world. The current trajectory, if unchanged, will result in the irreversible loss of thousands of species and the ecosystem services on which billions of people depend.

Required actions include: halting deforestation and land conversion; reforming agricultural production to be nature-positive; ending overfishing and reforming fisheries management; aggressively reducing greenhouse gas emissions; addressing pollution; and scaling investment in protected areas, habitat restoration, and sustainable species management.

The report emphasizes that biodiversity loss and climate change are deeply interconnected crises that must be addressed together. Protecting and restoring nature is among the most cost-effective strategies available for both climate mitigation and adaptation.
